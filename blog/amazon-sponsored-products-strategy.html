Understanding Campaign Types

Before we dive into strategy, let's clarify the three types of Amazon Sponsored Products campaigns:

1. Sponsored Products (Keyword-Based)

Bid on specific search terms. These appear in search results and on product pages. Best for: New product launches, high-margin items, building keyword rankings.

2. Sponsored Products (Product Targeting)

Target specific ASINs or categories. These appear on product detail pages. Best for: Competitor conquesting, complementary products, retargeting.

3. Sponsored Brands (formerly Headline Search Ads)

Brand-focused ads with your logo and multiple products. Only available to brand-registered sellers. Best for: Brand awareness, protecting your brand terms.

The 5-Phase Amazon PPC Strategy

Here's the framework I recommend for all sellers:

Phase 1: Foundation (Weeks 1-2)

Goal: Gather data without losing money

  • Create auto-targeting campaigns (broad match)
  • Set bids at Amazon's recommended level
  • Daily budget: $20-50 per campaign
  • Let campaigns run for 14 days minimum

Why auto campaigns first? They help you discover which keywords convert without upfront research. Amazon's algorithm also learns what your product is, which helps in later phases.

Phase 2: Keyword Research (Week 3)

Goal: Extract high-performing keywords from auto campaigns

  • Download search term reports
  • Identify keywords with:
  • ACoS under 30% (or your target)
  • At least 10 clicks
  • At least 1 sale

Phase 3: Structured Campaigns (Week 4)

Goal: Control bids at granular level

Create separate campaigns for each match type:

  • Campaign 1: Exact match keywords
  • Campaign 2: Phrase match keywords
  • Campaign 3: Broad match keywords

This structure lets you bid differently based on conversion probability. Exact matches should get your highest bids; broad matches your lowest.

Bidding Strategies That Work

Dynamic Bidding (Down Only)

Amazon lowers your bid by up to 50% when a conversion is unlikely. Best for: Sellers with healthy margins who want to protect against waste.

Dynamic Bidding (Up and Down)

Amazon can increase your bid by up to 100% when conversion is likely. Best for: Competitive keywords where you don't want to miss sales.

Fixed Bids

Your bid stays exactly where you set it. Best for: Fine-tuned campaigns where you've done the research.

My Recommendation: Fixed Bids for Control

While dynamic bidding sounds attractive, I've found fixed bids with manual adjustments gives better long-term results. You have full control over spend and can respond to seasonal changes faster.

Common Sponsored Products Mistakes

  1. Running too many campaigns: Start with 3-5 campaigns, master them, then expand
  2. Not using negative keywords: Without negatives, you're wasting budget on irrelevant searches
  3. Basing decisions on too little data: Wait for at least 40 clicks before deciding a keyword doesn't work
  4. Ignoring product targeting: Competitor ASIN targeting often has better ROAS than keywords
  5. Setting and forgetting: PPC requires weekly optimization to maintain performance

Advanced Tactics

Campaign Cannibalization

If you're running both auto and manual campaigns for the same products, your manual campaigns will steal impressions from auto. Solution: Put different products in each campaign type, or accept that manual will win the auctions.

Dayparting

Some sellers find that certain hours convert better. Use Amazon's campaign scheduling (available in campaign settings) to adjust bids by time of day.

Competitor Conquesting

Target your competitors' best sellers with product targeting. Offer better value (price, reviews, Prime eligibility) and let their traffic see your ad.

FAQ: Amazon Sponsored Products

What's a good ROAS for Amazon PPC?

It depends on your margins, but generally: 1x = break even, 2x = decent, 3x+ = excellent. High-margin products (60%+) can thrive at lower ROAS; low-margin products (30% or less) need 4-5x ROAS to be profitable.

How long until I see results from PPC?

Initial data comes in 1-2 weeks, but meaningful optimization requires 4-6 weeks of data. Don't make major changes based on the first week's results.

Should I run PPC on unprofitable products?

Sometimes yes, strategically. If a product drives cross-sells or helps your overall BSR, it might be worth running at break-even or slightly below. Just track it carefully.

When should I pause keywords?

Pause keywords that have: (a) 50+ clicks and 0 sales, (b) ACoS 3x above target after sufficient data, (c) High spend but declining conversion trend.
