Amazon Pay-Per-Click (PPC) advertising is the most powerful tool for driving immediate sales on the platform. Unlike organic SEO which takes months to build, well-optimized PPC campaigns can generate sales from day one.

In this comprehensive guide, you'll learn everything you need to know about Amazon PPC in 2026—from campaign types to bidding strategies to advanced optimization techniques.

What is Amazon PPC?

Amazon PPC allows sellers to advertise their products within Amazon's search results and product pages. You pay a fee only when a shopper clicks on your ad, hence "pay-per-click."

The benefits include:

  • Immediate visibility — Your products appear at the top of search results instantly
  • Sales acceleration — Generate sales while building organic rankings
  • Keyword data — Learn which search terms convert into sales
  • Competitor targeting — Appear alongside or above competing products

Types of Amazon PPC Campaigns

1. Sponsored Products

Sponsored Products are the most common PPC format. They appear within search results and on product detail pages, promoting individual product listings.

2. Sponsored Brands

Sponsored Brands ads showcase your brand logo and up to 3 products. They appear at the top of search results and create brand awareness alongside product promotion.

Requirements: You must be a brand registered with Amazon Brand Registry.

3. Sponsored Display

Sponsored Display reaches shoppers both on and off Amazon by displaying ads on product detail pages, customer review pages, and category pages. You can also target audiences based on shopping behaviors.

Campaign Structure Best Practices

A well-structured PPC campaign separates products by:

  • Match types — Group campaigns by Broad, Phrase, and Exact matches
  • Product categories — Separate campaigns by product type or category
  • Performance tiers — Create separate campaigns for top performers vs. testing
  • Keyword volume — High-volume keywords in one campaign, low-volume in another

Keyword Match Types Explained

Exact Match

Syntax: [keyword]

Your ad appears when someone searches for that exact keyword or close variations (singular/plural, common misspellings).

Example: [running shoes] would match: "running shoes", "running shoe", "runing shoes"

Phrase Match

Syntax: "keyword"

Your ad appears when someone searches containing your keyword in that exact phrase.

Example: "running shoes" would match: "best running shoes", "running shoes for men", "women's running shoes"

Broad Match

Syntax: keyword

Your ad appears for searches related to your keyword, even if the exact phrase isn't present.

Example: running shoes would match: "jogging footwear", "athletic shoes", "marathon sneakers"

Bidding Strategies

Dynamic Bidding (Down Only)

Amazon lowers your bid by up to 50% for clicks unlikely to convert. Use when you're focused on improving ACoS on established products.

Dynamic Bidding (Up and Down)

Amazon can increase your bid by up to 100% for clicks more likely to convert. Good for new products or when testing keywords.

Fixed Bids

You bid exactly what you set. Use when you want precise control over spending per keyword.

Bid Plus

Automatically increases your bid by up to 50% when you're winning the Buy Box. Great for products with high conversion rates.

ACoS vs. RoAS

ACoS (Advertising Cost of Sales) = What percentage of sales you spend on ads

RoAS (Return on Ad Spend) = How much revenue you generate per dollar spent

Example: If you spend $50 on ads and generate $200 in sales:

  • ACoS = $50 / $200 = 25%
  • RoAS = $200 / $50 = 4x

Optimization Checklist

Weekly Tasks

  1. Review search term reports for new high-performing keywords
  2. Pause underperforming keywords (high spend, low/no sales)
  3. Adjust bids based on ACoS targets
  4. Check for negative keyword opportunities

Monthly Tasks

  1. Analyze campaign performance by match type
  2. Test new keyword variations
  3. Review competitor ad presence
  4. Adjust campaign structure based on learnings

Common PPC Mistakes to Avoid

  • Not using negative keywords — Wasting budget on irrelevant searches
  • Setting and forgetting — PPC requires ongoing optimization
  • Bidding too low — If you're not winning impressions, increase bids
  • Ignoring search terms — The data tells you what's actually converting
  • Poor product selection for testing — Only advertise products with strong images and reviews
